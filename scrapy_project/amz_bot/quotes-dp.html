IamOneill
5.0 out of 5 starsA deep and engrossing read
February 2, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition|Verified Purchase
I write this review in the knowledge that probably no one will read it - there are already over 700 reviews and almost all of them are five star. This review is part of that legion and that's fine by me.

So given I am writing this review for me - I will take some licence. Firstly I have read loads of PA ficton ( favorite so far is The Old Man and the Wasteland), but I hesitated with my purchase of Swan Song. I kept coming across references and praise of Swan Song. At $10 is was a lot more than many Kindle reads I buy. Then I thought, the last few books I read were only a couple of bucks - but they were c#%^ and pretty short too. So I thought, Swan Song is over 800 pages - so even if it is just okay it is still better value. So I bought it and have not experienced buyers remorse.

Swan Song draws you in. It is a different type of PA novel as it as a mystical undertone. Not in the kind of 'roll your eyes, give me a break' way - but a spiritual side to the story that adds to its depth and humanity. There are several concurrent plots which weave together as the story unfolds. Each sub plot is interesting. What surprised me was that I became so engrossed with one story line, I literally forgot about some other characters and was surprised when they reappeared. It was ' oh of course, I had completly forgotten about you guys'. That is a really good thing showing just how easy it is to become lost in the story.

If I was being ultra critical I would say that there was a certain 'convenience' of events towards the end. Also - and yes this is just a personal bugbear, the author has obviously had limited exposure to horses. Horses do not lap water like a dog - they suck it. They also really don't behave in the way Mule did. Yes, yes I know it is a minor point - but it was one of the few times in the story where an inaccuracy brought me out of the story and back to reality.

I see many reviewers have compared this to other books ( and no I am not going to reference the books). This really gets my goat. Swan Song is an original work that stands high in its own right. It is , in my opinion, almost insulting to say 'its just like xyz', 'it's as good as xyz'. Swan Song is a great read - enough said.

scott
1.0 out of 5 starsDisasater Porn meets The Dukes of Hazzard
July 3, 2013
Format: Kindle Edition
With "Swan Song" Robert McCammon achieves the unenviable position of being at the very top of my "worst books ever written" list. That he does so with a novel dealing with the end of the world, and a promise of redemption is no small achievement either--as I am a huge fan of the end of the world genre. Whether we go back to the original classics like "The Beach" or "Alas, Babylon" or enjoy lesser known but triumphant works such as "This Is The Way The World Ends" (by Morrow), or even stick with a recent but spectacular example like "The Road" all of these works have the promise of inspiration, struggle, tragedy, and triumph. Never has a novel so completely failed to realize this promise as has "Swan Song".

Dull, leaden, and uninspired from start to end the work has not a single characteristic that I find commendable or worthwhile.

To start with the most obvious, let me explain the title of this review. I call this Disaster Porn, because like pornography the depiction of the end of the world, and the subsequent horrors endured by many of the characters, serves no purpose but to titilate. There is no moral lesson here, no profound insight into nature or human nature, and not an iota of character development occurs as a result. Even more astonishingly, the various horrors described in the novel, invariably in dull and plodding prose, do not advance the plot either. They simply exist for the sake of depicting misery. And thus the second comparison I evoke: The Dukes of Hazzard. Just like the inevitable car chase, these events exist for no other reason than, in the '70s, car chases had to exist. They didn't develop character, advance plot, nor were they really that entertaining. (Which is why we don't see them in TV cop shows anymore.) So goes the first half of the book: it exist much like a '70s TV car chase: for its own sake.

Now if the horror were particularly chilling, or scary, or poignant, or even well written, this would perhaps be excusable. Perhaps. But it is not. It is universally dull, uninteresting, and written with the vocabulary of an eighth or ninth grader. The text is bereft of metaphor, simile, or poetry. I believe the print version runs to nearly 1000 pages, and in all this text I counted 6 similes (and not a single, lonely metaphor). The fact that it is so unrelievedly insipid and dull became the only challenge to finishing the book--I had to see if the author could sustain his crime against literature for all 1000 pages, or if in fact he might slip and inadvertently elevate his writing to the mediocre. (I should say in passing that my condemnation does not come from one who does not enjoy the horror genre either--again, I have read a reasonable amount of horror, old and new, and not once does McCammon actually present a situation that he is capable of infusing with anything approaching horror. Pathos aplenty. Horror, alas, none.)

Again this backdrop, characters that are dumber, more simplistic, and less interesting versions of those found in Stephen King's "The Stand" lurch their way towards the end of the book. With only two exceptions (Roland Croninger and Jimbo) none of these characters develops or changes at all over the course of the novel (and seven years of nuclear winter). Nor do they drive the plot in any substantial way. To McCammon, a character is not somebody who does something out of necessity, because their nature demands that they do. To McCammon a character seems only to be a being to which things happen.

And there is so much more to dislike here. The Great Evil of the book (the Man of Many Faces) is revealed--minor spoiler here--to be a galactic evil that has existed before time and is just a malignant force of destruction and misery. But why does he visit Earth? Does he have any relationship to a devil or The Devil? Why does he hate himself? Why is he so powerless for a being which has existed for billions of years? None of this is revealed. Nor does the author give the slightest indication that he thinks such questions are interesting to either the reader or the characters of the novel. Because McCammon can't provide even the thinest explanation or pretext for the character, he becomes more plot device than character, and more deus ex machine than plot device. And so a great horror is revealed to be entirely, unintentionally, absurd.

Further, for a novel about the impact of nuclear winter, the world post apocalypse doest not ring true at all. After 7 years of nuclear winter where the temperature does not rise above freezing, there is apparently no more snow on the ground than after a good storm in todays world. And temperatures never get so cold that a couple sweaters and a good pair of mits are not enough to stay warm. Certainly roads remain passable. Batteries in cars never die from misuse. People never suffer from radiation poisoning (pretty incredible that the world has been annihilated by nuclear bombs and yet radiation sickness is nowhere to be found). Numerous items such as these, both large and small, make no sense and stand no test of logic or reason. Virtually the entire novel takes place in winter and I cannot believe that Mr. McCammon has ever been cold. I couldn't decide whether to find it unintentionally funny or just irritating that he claims that nuclear bombs dropped at the poles could knock the Earth of it's axis. (This wouldnt happen even if all the nuclear bombs in the world were detonated at one of the poles. But suppose that were not true however, and instead suppose that bombs could move the Earth of it's poles if they were detonated at both poles. Well then--if they were dropped at BOTH poles--the forces would cancel each other out. This is basic physics. Well within Newton's grasp--and most everybody since. But basic science like this elludes McCammon at every turn.

And so, inevitably, the book draws to a close. Along the way, it manages not a single redeeming feature. The characters live (and die) and we do not care. They never achieve status as archetypes. The never elevate the struggle to a universal one between good and evil. They just stumble along, and do exactly as we would expect them to do from the very first time they are introduced. There is nothing to redeem the tedium, unless we are sadists and desire (disaster) porn for it's own sake. And like the Dukes of Hazzard, this novel is better put behind us and forgotten, never to be seen again, not even on cable in reruns.
